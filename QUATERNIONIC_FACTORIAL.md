# Quaternionic Factorial Foundation

## Purpose

This document fixes the conservative mathematical meaning of the quaternionic factorial used in Quaternionic Spectral Geometry.

The scalar quaternionic factorial is studied before any anchor-generating prefactor, well potential, or quantization interpretation is introduced. The first questions are:

- what is already inherited from the complex Gamma function,
- what depends only on the scalar and imaginary radius of a quaternion,
- what changes when the imaginary axis varies,
- where noncommutative composition first appears,
- and how the construction may be extended to operators on QSG state spaces.

## Definition

Let

$$
q=a+rI,
\qquad
I^2=-1,
\qquad
r\ge 0,
$$

where $I$ is a unit imaginary quaternion. For $\operatorname{Re}(q)=a>-1$, define

$$
\mathfrak F(q)
=
\int_0^\infty e^{-x}x^q\,dx,
$$

using

$$
x^q=e^{q\log x}.
$$

Because $x>0$ and $\log x$ is real,

$$
x^{a+rI}
=
x^a\left(\cos(r\log x)+I\sin(r\log x)\right).
$$

Therefore

$$
\mathfrak F(a+rI)
=
A(a,r)+I B(a,r),
$$

where

$$
A(a,r)
=
\int_0^\infty e^{-x}x^a\cos(r\log x)\,dx,
$$

and

$$
B(a,r)
=
\int_0^\infty e^{-x}x^a\sin(r\log x)\,dx.
$$

Within the complex slice $\mathbb C_I=\{u+Iv:u,v\in\mathbb R\}$,

$$
\boxed{\mathfrak F(q)=\Gamma(q+1)}.
$$

This identity is the scalar baseline. It means that a single evaluation does not by itself define a new special-function spectrum beyond the intrinsic slice extension of the complex Gamma function.

## Axis Covariance

For fixed $a$ and $r$, let $\hat n$ be any unit imaginary quaternion. Then

$$
\mathfrak F(a+r\hat n)
=
A(a,r)+\hat n B(a,r).
$$

The quantities

$$
A(a,r),
\qquad
B(a,r),
\qquad
|\mathfrak F(a+r\hat n)|
=
\sqrt{A(a,r)^2+B(a,r)^2}
$$

are independent of the chosen axis $\hat n$. Changing $\hat n$ rotates the imaginary part without changing the scalar spectral amplitude.

Writing

$$
A+iB=R e^{i\theta},
$$

gives

$$
\mathfrak F(a+r\hat n)
=
R\left(\cos\theta+\hat n\sin\theta\right).
$$

Thus the scalar Gamma profile supplies a magnitude $R$ and phase angle $\theta$, while the quaternionic input supplies the oriented imaginary axis.

For a path $\hat n(\tau)$ on the sphere of imaginary units,

$$
\mathfrak F(a+r\hat n(\tau))
=
A+B\hat n(\tau).
$$

The output axis follows the input axis algebraically. A nontrivial geometric phase, parallel transport, or holonomy would require an additional connection or ordered transport rule; it is not generated by independent scalar evaluations alone.

## Identities Within One Slice

Because $q$ and $\mathfrak F(q)$ lie in the same commutative slice, the ordinary Gamma identities transfer directly wherever both sides are defined.

### Recurrence

Since $\mathfrak F(q)=\Gamma(q+1)$,

$$
\boxed{
\mathfrak F(q+1)
=
(q+1)\mathfrak F(q)
}
$$

or equivalently

$$
\boxed{
\mathfrak F(q)
=
q\,\mathfrak F(q-1).
}
$$

The expression $\mathfrak F(q+1)=q\mathfrak F(q)$ is not consistent with this indexing convention and should not be used.

### Conjugation

$$
\mathfrak F(\bar q)
=
\overline{\mathfrak F(q)}.
$$

Consequently,

$$
|\mathfrak F(\bar q)|
=
|\mathfrak F(q)|.
$$

### Reflection

Using $\Gamma(q+1)=q\Gamma(q)$ and the Euler reflection formula within one slice,

$$
\boxed{
\mathfrak F(q)\mathfrak F(-q)
=
\frac{\pi q}{\sin(\pi q)}
}
$$

where the terms are defined.

## Cross-Slice Composition

The first elementary noncommutative effect appears when factorial values associated with different imaginary axes are multiplied.

Let

$$
F_{\hat n}=A+B\hat n,
\qquad
F_{\hat m}=A+B\hat m,
$$

for unit imaginary quaternions $\hat n$ and $\hat m$. Their commutator is

$$
[F_{\hat n},F_{\hat m}]
=
F_{\hat n}F_{\hat m}-F_{\hat m}F_{\hat n}
=
2B^2(\hat n\times\hat m),
$$

where the cross product identifies the imaginary quaternion subspace with $\mathbb R^3$.

For orthogonal axes $I$ and $J$ with $IJ=K$,

$$
[F_I,F_J]
=
2B^2K.
$$

The normalized commutator is

$$
\frac{\|[F_{\hat n},F_{\hat m}]\|}
{\|F_{\hat n}\|\,\|F_{\hat m}\|}
=
\frac{2B^2}{A^2+B^2}
\,\|\hat n\times\hat m\|.
$$

This value can approach $2$ when the Gamma value is nearly pure imaginary and the axes are orthogonal.

This does not mean that a single scalar factorial is intrinsically noncommutative. It means that ordered compositions of values carried by different quaternionic orientations retain information that is absent from their norms alone.

## Numerical Baseline

A reproducible experiment is provided in

[`experiments/quaternionic_factorial_slices.py`](experiments/quaternionic_factorial_slices.py).

For the path

$$
q=\frac12+rI,
\qquad
0\le r\le 15,
$$

it checks:

- agreement between the defining integral and $\Gamma(q+1)$,
- axis-independence of the norm,
- rotation of the imaginary output with the input axis,
- recurrence, conjugation, and reflection identities,
- and the commutator between orthogonal slice values.

The observed behavior is:

1. $|\Gamma(3/2+ir)|$ decays rapidly as $r$ increases.
2. The slice phase continues to wind while the magnitude decays.
3. Rotating the imaginary axis rotates only the imaginary vector of the output.
4. Cross-slice normalized commutators can be large even when the absolute factorial amplitudes are small.

These are numerical illustrations of the analytic formulas, not evidence of new particles, resonance wells, or quantized scales.

## Operator-Valued Direction

The more consequential QSG question is whether the factorial can be applied to an operator whose spectral components carry quaternionic orientation.

Formally, one may ask for

$$
\mathfrak F(T)=\Gamma(T+1),
$$

where $T$ is a quaternionic linear operator or a complex matrix representation of one. Such a definition requires a stated functional calculus, operator domain, and spectral restrictions.

A candidate QSG operator may have the schematic form

$$
T
=
a(s)\mathbf 1
+
b_1(s)L_1
+
b_2(s)L_2
+
b_3(s)L_3,
$$

where $L_1,L_2,L_3$ generate an $SU(2)$ action on truncated $S^3$ harmonics.

The research questions are then:

- whether $\Gamma(T+1)$ preserves the intended state space,
- how its spectrum depends on the $SU(2)$ representation,
- whether different ordered axis paths produce distinct operators,
- how $[\Gamma(T_1+1),\Gamma(T_2+1)]$ depends on noncommuting generators,
- and whether any resulting invariant differs from ordinary complex matrix functional calculus.

No physical conclusion should be drawn until these operator questions are made precise.

## Relation To AGQF

The Anchor-Generating Quaternionic Factorial is not part of the scalar definition above. It adds an external prefactor or potential intended to create wells or anchor locations.

The canonical order of study is:

$$
\text{scalar quaternionic factorial}
\longrightarrow
\text{cross-slice composition}
\longrightarrow
\text{operator-valued factorial}
\longrightarrow
\text{optional anchored extensions}.
$$

AGQF should remain a downstream conjectural model and should not be used to define the primitive meaning of the quaternionic factorial or the scale coordinate.

## Claim Status

### Standard background

- Euler's Gamma integral and its recurrence, conjugation, and reflection identities.
- Complex slices $\mathbb C_I$ inside $\mathbb H$.
- Quaternion multiplication and the commutator of pure imaginary directions.
- Analytic and intrinsic functional calculi for suitable quaternionic operators.

### QSG definitions

- $\mathfrak F(q)=\int_0^\infty e^{-x}x^q\,dx$ with the indexing convention $\mathfrak F(q)=\Gamma(q+1)$.
- The use of axis covariance and cross-slice ordered products as QSG diagnostics.

### Prototype questions

- Applying $\Gamma(T+1)$ to finite $SU(2)$-aware operators.
- Comparing pointwise products, slice products, and operator composition.
- Identifying basis-independent invariants of axis-varying factorial paths.

### Conjectural extensions

- Interpreting factorial phase or commutators as physical coherence.
- Deriving observed spectra or quantization from the factorial.
- Adding anchor-generating factors or scale-well potentials.

## References And Distinctions

- Gentili and Stoppato, *A local representation formula for quaternionic slice regular functions*, provides background on slice regularity and representation across quaternionic slices.
- Gantner, *Operator Theory on One-Sided Quaternionic Linear Spaces: Intrinsic S-Functional Calculus and Spectral Operators*, develops intrinsic functional calculus for quaternionic operators.
- Vasilescu, *Spectrum and Analytic Functional Calculus in Real and Quaternionic Frameworks*, gives another operator-calculus route.
- Burnol, *Quaternionic Gamma functions and their logarithmic derivatives as spectral functions*, studies quaternionic local-field Gamma factors in harmonic analysis. That construction is mathematically distinct from the scalar slice factorial defined here and should not be conflated with it.
